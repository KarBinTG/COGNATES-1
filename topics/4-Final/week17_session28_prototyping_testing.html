Stage 4: Prototype 🛠️

Prototype: A simplified, testable version of a solution. NOT the final product. Purpose: learn fast, fail cheap.

A Prototype IS:

  • Quick to build (minutes to hours)
  • Cheap (paper, free tools)
  • Testable (user can interact)
  • Disposable (expect to rebuild)
  • Focused on ONE question

A Prototype is NOT:

  • The final product
  • Polished or pretty
  • Feature-complete
  • Expensive to create
  • Something you're attached to

5 Prototyping Principles

1
Build to LEARN, not to keep. Expect to throw it away.
2
Start ROUGH. Don't polish early. Ugly is fine.
3
Make it TESTABLE. Users must interact with it.
4
ONE question per prototype. What's the critical assumption?
5
Fail fast, learn faster. Every failed prototype saves months.

Paper Prototyping: How To

Steps

  1. Draw each SCREEN on separate paper
  2. Label buttons & interactive elements
  3. Create arrows showing flow
  4. Add sticky notes for pop-ups/menus
  5. YOU act as the "computer" during testing

Why It Works

  • 5 minutes to build = low cost of failure
  • Users focus on CONCEPT not visuals
  • Easy to modify (just redraw)
  • No tech skills needed
  • Zappos started this way → $1B company

Stage 5: Test 🧪

Test with TARGET users (not friends/family). Their confusion is your most valuable data.

1
Give prototype to user. No explanation.
2
Observe SILENTLY. Don't guide, don't help.
3
Ask: "What do you think this does?" "What's confusing?"
4
Note: where they struggle, what they misunderstand
5
Ask: "Would you use this? Why or why not?"

Testing: Do's & Don'ts

✅ DO

  • Test with 5+ users (finds 85% of problems)
  • Ask open-ended questions
  • "Tell me what you're thinking"
  • Record OBSERVATIONS, not just opinions
  • Test with TARGET users (who have the problem)
  • Watch body language and hesitation

❌ DON'T

  • Explain your design during test
  • Ask leading questions ("isn't this easy?")
  • Take feedback personally
  • Test with groupmates/family (too nice)
  • Defend your design when criticized
  • Change the prototype MID-test

Iteration: Learn & Improve 🔄

After testing, analyze results and decide next action:

✅ Works Well

Keep & refine that part. Build on success.

😕 Confusing

Redesign that specific element. Test again.

❌ Wrong Problem

Go BACK to Empathize/Define. Reframe.

💡 New Insight

Pivot or adjust approach based on discovery.

Goal ≠ get it right first time. Goal = LEARN as fast as possible. Every loop makes it better.

MVP: Minimum Viable Product

The simplest version that delivers core value AND allows learning from real users.

❌ Too Little

Landing page only. Can't test anything meaningful.

✅ MVP (Just Right)

Core feature works. Solves ONE problem. Gets real feedback.

❌ Too Much

Full product, all features. Months wasted if wrong.

Facebook MVP (2004): Profile + wall + friend list. Harvard only. That's it.

Dropbox MVP: Just a video showing the concept. No actual product yet.

🛠️ Workshop: Build & Test (Graded)

Same groups. Take your TOP idea from last session.

Step 1 (10 min):

Create paper prototype – at least 4 screens, main flow

Step 2 (5 min):

Test with ANOTHER group – give silently, observe

Step 3 (3 min):

Note feedback – what confused? what worked?

Step 4 (5 min):

Iterate – improve design based on feedback

Present (2 min):

Original → Feedback → Improved version

⏱️ Total: ~25 min. Papers and pens ready. GO!

Key Takeaways

1.

Prototypes are for LEARNING, not shipping.

2.

Start rough, test fast. Paper = 5 min, reveals 80% of problems.

3.

Test with TARGET users, not friends/family.

4.

Stay SILENT during testing. Confusion = valuable data.

5.

Iterate: improve based on what you learn each round.

6.

MVP = simplest version with core value + real feedback.

7.

Zappos, Dropbox, Airbnb all started with tiny MVPs.
