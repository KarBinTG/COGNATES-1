What Is Cloud Computing?

Cloud Computing (NIST Definition)

A model for enabling ubiquitous, convenient, on-demand network access to a shared pool of configurable computing resources (networks, servers, storage, applications, services) that can be rapidly provisioned and released with minimal management effort.

07

Cloud = Renting, Not Buying

Instead of buying your own computer/server, you RENT computing power over the internet and pay only for what you use.

Like electricity: you don't build a power plant. You plug in and pay for what you consume.

08

The Utility Analogy

Electricity

  • Don't build power plant
  • Plug in when needed
  • Pay per kilowatt used
  • Provider maintains everything
  • Scale up/down freely

Cloud Computing

  • Don't buy servers
  • Connect when needed
  • Pay per hour/GB used
  • Provider maintains hardware
  • Scale up/down instantly

What makes cloud computing "cloud" (per NIST)

10

NIST: 5 Characteristics

#CharacteristicMeaning
1On-demand self-serviceGet resources instantly without calling anyone
2Broad network accessAccess from any device, anywhere (phone, laptop)
3Resource poolingMany users share same infrastructure
4Rapid elasticityScale up/down instantly based on demand
5Measured servicePay only for what you actually use
11

1. On-Demand Self-Service

Need a server? Click a button, have it in 60 seconds. No purchase orders, no waiting for delivery, no IT department approval needed.

Example: A student creates a free Google Drive account and instantly gets 15GB of cloud storage. No hardware purchase. No setup. Instant.

12

2. Broad Network Access

Access your files and applications from ANY device with internet. Phone, tablet, laptop, desktop, smart TV. Same data, everywhere.

Example: Start a Google Doc on your laptop in class, continue editing on your phone at home, present from classroom PC. Same file, synced everywhere.

13

3. Resource Pooling

Cloud providers serve MILLIONS of users from shared infrastructure. You don't know (or care) which specific server holds your data. It's abstracted away.

Like a hotel: many guests share the same building, pool, and lobby. You get your private room, but the building is shared. Efficient for everyone.

14

4. Rapid Elasticity

Scale UP when demand spikes, scale DOWN when quiet. Automatically. No wasted resources.

Example: Shopee on 11.11 sale: traffic 100x normal. Cloud auto-scales to handle millions of shoppers. After sale? Scales back down. They only pay for the surge hours.

15

5. Measured Service

Pay-as-you-go. Like a taxi meter: running = paying. Stopped = not paying. Use 2 hours of compute? Pay for 2 hours. Not 24.

Traditional: Buy ₱500,000 server, use it 4 hours/day = waste.
Cloud: Pay only for 4 hours/day of compute = efficient.

IaaS: Infrastructure as a Service

Rent raw computing resources: servers, storage, networking. YOU install and manage everything on top.

Provider ManagesYou ManageExamples
Physical servers, networking, storage, virtualizationOS, middleware, apps, dataAWS EC2, Google Compute Engine, Azure VMs, DigitalOcean
20

PaaS: Platform as a Service

A complete development environment in the cloud. Write code, deploy, run. Don't worry about servers, OS, or infrastructure.

Provider ManagesYou ManageExamples
Servers, OS, middleware, runtime, databasesYour application code and dataHeroku, Google App Engine, Azure App Services, Railway
21

SaaS: Software as a Service

Complete applications delivered over the internet. Just open browser and use. No installation, no maintenance, no updates to worry about.

Provider ManagesYou ManageExamples
EVERYTHING: servers, OS, app, updates, securityJust your data and settingsGmail, Google Docs, Zoom, Canva, Shopify, Salesforce

Public vs. Private

Public Cloud

  • Shared with millions of users
  • Cheap (economies of scale)
  • No maintenance needed
  • Less control
  • Data on shared servers

Private Cloud

  • Dedicated to ONE organization
  • Expensive to build/maintain
  • Full control
  • Maximum security
  • Meets compliance requirements
27

Hybrid Cloud: Best of Both

Most large organizations use hybrid: keep sensitive data in private cloud, run public-facing services on public cloud.

Example: BDO (Philippine bank)
Private cloud: customer account data, transactions (security/compliance)
Public cloud: website, mobile app interface, marketing (scalability/cost)
