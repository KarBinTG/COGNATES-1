The Law

RA 10175: Cybercrime Prevention Act

Enacted September 12, 2012. Defines cybercrimes, prescribes penalties, and establishes enforcement mechanisms. Covers offenses against confidentiality, integrity, and availability of computer data and systems.

03 Part 3

Incident
Response

What to do when you get hacked

15

IR: 6 Steps

StepActionExample
1. PreparationPlan BEFORE incident happensWrite IR plan, assign team roles
2. IdentificationDetect that something is wrongAlert: unusual login from Nigeria
3. ContainmentStop the spreadDisable compromised account immediately
4. EradicationRemove the threatDelete malware, patch vulnerability
5. RecoveryRestore normal operationsRestore from backup, verify clean
6. Lessons LearnedPrevent recurrenceDocument what happened, update procedures
16

Personal IR: Account Hacked

  • 1. Change password IMMEDIATELY (from a different device)
  • 2. Enable MFA if not already on
  • 3. Check "active sessions" and log out all others
  • 4. Check if other accounts use the same password (change those too)
  • 5. Report to the platform (Facebook, Google, GCash)
  • 6. If money involved: file report with PNP Anti-Cybercrime Group

Zero Trust Security

"Never Trust, Always Verify"

Traditional: trust everyone inside the network (castle-and-moat).
Zero Trust: trust NO ONE by default, verify EVERY request regardless of location. Even employees inside the office must authenticate for every action.

AI + Cybercrime

  • Deepfake voice calls: AI clones your boss's voice asking for wire transfer
  • AI-written phishing: perfect grammar, personalized, no red flags
  • Automated vulnerability scanning: AI finds and exploits weaknesses faster
  • Defense: AI also used for detection (fight AI with AI)
  • The arms race: both attackers and defenders use AI

Getting Started

  • TryHackMe / HackTheBox: free hands-on practice
  • CTF competitions: Capture The Flag cybersecurity contests
  • Bug bounty programs: get PAID to find vulnerabilities
  • 3.5M unfilled cybersecurity jobs globally (massive demand)
26

Ethical Hacking vs Crime

Ethical (Legal)

  • Permission from system owner
  • Written agreement/contract
  • Report findings responsibly
  • Goal: improve security
  • Career: pentester, bug bounty

Criminal (Illegal)

  • No permission
  • No authorization
  • Exploit for personal gain
  • Goal: steal, damage, extort
  • Consequence: prison (RA 10175)

Case Study: Philippine Bank

Bangladesh Bank Heist (2016)

Hackers broke into Bangladesh Bank's SWIFT system (used for international transfers). They routed the stolen money through PHILIPPINE BANKS (RCBC Makati branch). $81 million successfully stolen. Philippines was the laundering destination. Led to major banking security reforms in PH.

29

Security Culture

  • Security is everyone's responsibility, not just IT department
  • Regular training: keep employees aware of new threats
  • Simulated phishing: test if employees fall for fake attacks
  • Clear policies: what's allowed, what's not, consequences
  • Reward reporting: people who report suspicious activity = heroes

Activity: Incident Response Drill

Scenario Response

Your school receives a ransomware attack. All student records are encrypted. The attacker demands ₱500,000 in Bitcoin.

In groups, plan your response:
1. Immediate actions (first 30 minutes)
2. Who do you contact? (internal + external)
3. Should you pay the ransom? Why/why not?
4. How to recover without paying?
5. What policies would prevent this in the future?
