01 Part 1

Linear vs
Circular

Take-Make-Waste vs Design-Use-Return

05

Linear Economy (Current)

Take → Make → Use → Waste

Extract raw materials → manufacture product → consumer uses → throw away. This model assumes infinite resources and infinite capacity for waste. Neither is true. The tech industry is one of the worst offenders: planned obsolescence, sealed batteries, non-repairable designs.

06

Circular Economy (Alternative)

Design → Use → Return → Remanufacture → Reuse

Products designed to last, be repaired, upgraded, refurbished, and eventually recycled into new materials. Waste is designed OUT of the system. Every "end" becomes a new beginning. Mimics nature: no waste in ecosystems.

07

Comparison

Linear (Bad)

  • Mine → Make → Dispose
  • Planned obsolescence
  • Sealed, non-repairable
  • Upgrade = buy new
  • Profit from volume
  • Resources deplete

Circular (Better)

  • Design → Use → Return → Reuse
  • Designed for longevity
  • Modular, repairable
  • Upgrade = replace one part
  • Profit from service/durability
  • Resources cycle endlessly

10

Right-to-Repair Movement

  • Consumers should be able to repair devices they OWN
  • Manufacturers must provide: parts, tools, repair manuals
  • EU: passed right-to-repair law (2024), mandatory spare parts for 5-10 years
  • Apple: forced to adopt USB-C (EU regulation), now sells self-repair kits
  • iFixit: rates devices on "repairability score" (Fairphone: 10/10, AirPods: 0/10)
  • Philippines: no right-to-repair law yet, but growing awareness
11

Case Study: Fairphone

The World's Most Repairable Phone

Fairphone 5: Modular design, every component replaceable with a screwdriver. Battery pops out. Screen snaps off. Camera module upgradeable. 8+ years of software updates promised. Made with fair-trade materials. Repairability: 10/10 (iFixit). Proof that sustainable phones CAN exist.

Compare: iPhone repairability score = 4/10. Galaxy = 5/10. Fairphone = 10/10.

15

Case: Framework Laptop

Framework: A laptop designed from scratch for upgradeability. Swap ports (USB-C, HDMI, SD card modules). Upgrade RAM, storage, screen, keyboard, even motherboard. Same chassis, upgraded internals. Buy a laptop ONCE, upgrade parts for 10+ years. Currently: $999 base, growing community, proving the concept works commercially.

17

Greenwashing Warning

Not All "Green" Claims Are Real

Greenwashing = making misleading environmental claims to appear sustainable without making real changes. Examples: "eco-friendly" packaging that's not recyclable, "carbon neutral" claims based on cheap offsets, "sustainable" products still designed to break. Always look for SPECIFIC, MEASURABLE commitments.

18

⏸

Spot Greenwashing

A company says "we plant a tree for every phone sold" but their phones have glued batteries and no spare parts. Real sustainability or greenwashing? (Greenwashing: the core product design is still wasteful)
